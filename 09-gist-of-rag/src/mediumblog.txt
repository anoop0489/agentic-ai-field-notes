Vector Databases: A Practical Introduction

This is an original sample document written for this repository so the RAG
ingestion demo has something to embed and index. It intentionally avoids
copying any third-party article. Feel free to replace it with your own text.

What is a vector database?

A vector database is a system designed to store and search high-dimensional
vectors efficiently. In modern AI applications, text, images, and audio are
converted into numeric vectors called embeddings. Two pieces of content that
are similar in meaning end up close together in this vector space, even if they
share no exact keywords. A vector database indexes these embeddings so that,
given a query vector, it can quickly return the most similar stored vectors.

Why do we need them for LLM applications?

Large language models are trained on a fixed snapshot of data and cannot see
your private documents. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) solves this by
looking up relevant passages from your own corpus at question time and feeding
them into the prompt. The retrieval step depends on a vector database: you embed
your documents once, store the vectors, and then embed each incoming question to
find the closest passages. This grounds the answer in real source text.

How similarity search works

Most vector databases use an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) algorithm.
Comparing a query against every stored vector exactly would be too slow at
scale, so ANN indexes such as HNSW or IVF trade a tiny amount of accuracy for a
large gain in speed. Similarity is commonly measured with cosine similarity,
which looks at the angle between two vectors rather than their magnitude.

Key concepts to remember

Embedding: a numeric vector that captures the meaning of a piece of content.
Dimensionality: the length of the vector, for example 1536 values.
Metric: the math used to compare vectors, such as cosine or dot product.
Index: the data structure that makes similarity search fast.
Metadata: extra fields stored alongside a vector, used for filtering results.

Choosing a vector database

Managed cloud services remove the burden of running infrastructure and scale
automatically. Self-hosted options give you full control and can run entirely
on your own machine. The right choice depends on your scale, budget, privacy
needs, and how much operational work you want to own.

Summary

Vector databases are the retrieval backbone of RAG systems. They turn the
abstract idea of semantic similarity into a fast, practical lookup, letting an
LLM answer questions about data it was never trained on.
